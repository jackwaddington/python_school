 Tyre Change Basics

1. Tire Types and Identification:
   - Definition: Tires are circular, flexible rubber covers that encase a wheel's rim to enable vehicle movement by adhering to a road surface. They come in various types such as radial, bias-ply, tubeless, and tubed tires.
   - How they work: Radial tires have the cords running perpendicular to the direction of travel, improving fuel efficiency, handling, and comfort. Bias-ply tires have the cords at an angle to the direction of travel, providing better traction but less fuel efficiency. Tubeless tires do not require an inner tube, reducing the risk of punctures and allowing a larger rim diameter for improved performance.
   - Concrete example: A typical passenger car uses radial tires with a size of P215/60R16, which means the tire has a width of 215 millimeters, an aspect ratio (height to width) of 60%, a rim diameter of 16 inches, and is a radial tire.

2. Tire Changing Process:
   - Definition: The procedure of removing and installing a tire from a wheel.
   - How they work: Start by loosening the lug nuts with a wrench before lifting the vehicle using a jack to support it securely. Remove the lug nuts completely, then lift the tire off the ground and pull it away from the wheel. Reverse the process for installation while reapplying the lug nuts finger-tight before lowering the vehicle and tightening them further with the wrench.
   - Concrete example: To change a flat tire on a car, first, place the jack under the frame near the tire you're going to work on. Loosen the lug nuts but do not remove them yet. Jack up the car until the tire is off the ground and remove the lug nuts. Lift the tire away from the wheel and replace it with the spare. Reverse the process, tightening the lug nuts finger-tight first before lowering the vehicle.

3. Tire Pressure:
   - Definition: The amount of air within a tire measured in pounds per square inch (psi) or kilopascals (kPa).
   - How they work: Proper tire pressure ensures even tire wear, fuel efficiency, and safety. It can be checked using a tire gauge.
   - Concrete example: For optimal performance, the recommended tire pressure for a typical passenger car is 32 psi or 2.2 bar (at room temperature).

4. Wheel Balancing:
   - Definition: The process of adjusting wheel weights to counteract unwanted vibrations and improve ride comfort caused by uneven weight distribution on the rim.
   - How they work: During the balancing process, the tire and wheel are spun while measuring its vibration levels at various points. Additional weights are added to heavy spots to reduce imbalances.
   - Concrete example: If a car's steering wheel is shaking after a tire change, it may require wheel balancing to eliminate the vibration. This can be done at an auto service center using specialized equipment designed for this purpose.
