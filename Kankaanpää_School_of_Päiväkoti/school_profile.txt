MATERIAL:
 Early Childhood Education in Päiväkoti: Core Concepts

1. **Play-Based Learning**
   - Definition: Play-based learning is an approach that encourages children to learn through active engagement in play activities, allowing them to explore, experiment, and develop their skills and knowledge in a fun and interactive manner.
   - How it works: The environment is set up with age-appropriate toys, materials, and resources that stimulate children's imagination, creativity, and problem-solving abilities. Teachers facilitate the learning process by guiding, supporting, and extending children's play experiences.
   - Concrete example: A teacher sets up a construction area filled with blocks, Legos, and other building materials. Children work together to build structures, solving problems related to balance, symmetry, and design.

2. **Whole-Child Development**
   - Definition: Whole-child development is an approach that focuses on the overall growth and development of a child, addressing their cognitive, emotional, social, physical, and creative needs.
   - How it works: Teachers create learning experiences that promote holistic development by integrating various areas of learning, such as math, language arts, science, art, and music, into play-based activities and routines.
   - Concrete example: A teacher plans a thematic unit on animals, incorporating storytelling, art projects, songs, outdoor explorations, and math games to engage children in learning about different animal species while also developing their language skills, creativity, motor coordination, and appreciation for nature.

3. **Culturally Responsive Teaching**
   - Definition: Culturally responsive teaching is an approach that recognizes the importance of incorporating students' cultural backgrounds, experiences, and identities into the learning process to create a more inclusive and equitable classroom environment.
   - How it works: Teachers learn about their students' cultural heritage, values, and beliefs, and integrate relevant content, perspectives, and teaching strategies into the curriculum. This helps students feel valued, respected, and empowered while promoting understanding and appreciation of diversity.
   - Concrete example: A teacher plans a lesson on traditional Finnish folktales, incorporating stories from various regions to represent the diverse cultural backgrounds of the students. The teacher also invites guest speakers who can share their personal experiences and traditions related to the stories being studied.

4. **Relationship-Based Pedagogy**
   - Definition: Relationship-based pedagogy is an approach that emphasizes building strong, positive relationships between teachers and children as a foundation for effective teaching and learning.
   - How it works: Teachers foster open communication, empathy, trust, and respect with students, creating a safe and supportive environment where children feel comfortable sharing their thoughts, feelings, and experiences. This helps build strong emotional bonds, promoting social-emotional development and enhancing the overall quality of the learning experience.
   - Concrete example: A teacher takes time each day to sit and talk with individual students, asking about their day, listening to their concerns, and offering support and encouragement. The teacher also creates opportunities for collaborative play and projects, helping children develop teamwork skills and a sense of community within the classroom.

EXAM QUESTIONS:

       C) An approach that recognizes the importance of incorporating students' cultural backgrounds into the learning process
       D) A pedagogy that emphasizes building strong, positive relationships between teachers and children
  Answer: A

  Q2: Which of the following is NOT a core concept in early childhood education in Päiväkoti?
       A) Whole-child development
       B) Play-based learning
       C) Work-based learning
       D) Relationship-based pedagogy
  Answer: C

MATERIAL:
 Finnish Language and Culture in Päiväkoti Context: Key Concepts

1. **Pöytää-ja-asioita (Tableware and Utensils)**
   - Definition: Pöytää-ja-asioita refers to the tableware, cutlery, and other utensils used during meals in a Finnish daycare center (Päiväkoti).
   - Function: They are essential for serving, eating, and handling food during meal times.
   - Example: Kuppi (cup), lasi (glass), ruoka-pöytä (dinner table), puukko (knife), kuiva-siipi (fork), and lentokone-siipi (spoon).

2. **Säännöt ja Tavoitteet (Rules and Goals)**
   - Definition: Säännöt ja tavoitteet refer to the rules and goals that guide the daily activities in a Finnish daycare center, promoting order, safety, and learning.
   - Function: They ensure a structured environment that supports children's development and well-being.
   - Example: A rule could be "Pöytään pistää kuppi" (Place the cup on the table). A goal could be encouraging independent eating skills in children.

3. **Joulupukki (Santa Claus)**
   - Definition: Joulupukki is a beloved character from Finnish folklore who brings Christmas presents to children.
   - Function: He symbolizes the Christmas spirit and plays a significant role in Finland's cultural traditions.
   - Example: Children write letters to Joulupukki, describing their wishes for Christmas gifts. On Christmas Eve, they expect him to visit their homes.

4. **Kansallisjuhlaviikko (National Week of Culture)**
   - Definition: Kansallisjuhlaviikko is an annual event in Finland that celebrates the country's culture, history, and traditions through various activities and events.
   - Function: It aims to educate and engage people in preserving and appreciating Finnish culture.
   - Example: Activities could include traditional craft workshops, storytelling sessions, music performances, and exhibitions showcasing Finnish art and history.

COURSE: Päiväkoti Curriculum Development
Required passes to complete: 19 weeks
────────────────────────────────────────
MATERIAL:
 Päiväkoti Curriculum Development

1. Early Childhood Education (ECE) Philosophies:
   - Reggio Emilia Approach: A child-centered, collaborative, and project-based learning approach that values children's potential and their role as active participants in the learning process. Example: A group project where children investigate and document their findings about caterpillars and their transformation into butterflies.
   - Montessori Method: An individualized educational approach focusing on self-directed activity, hands-on learning, and collaborative play. Example: A child choosing to work with a set of manipulative materials for developing number skills, such as the number rods or sandpaper numbers.
   - HighScope Approach: A research-based curriculum that encourages active learning, problem solving, and developmentally appropriate practices. Example: A teacher presents an objective, such as "building a tower," and the children plan, implement, and evaluate their solutions together.

2. Developmentally Appropriate Practice (DAP):
   - Definition: A framework that guides early childhood educators to design and implement learning experiences that are developmentally, culturally, linguistically, and individually appropriate for young children.
   - How it works: Educators consider the age, stage of development, interests, and learning styles of each child when planning and implementing lessons, activities, and assessments.
   - Concrete example: A teacher plans a nature walk that includes exploring different textures, shapes, and colors found in leaves, instead of simply leading the children on a walk without any specific focus or interactive elements.

3. Child-Initiated and Adult-Supported Play:
   - Definition: A balance between child-led exploration and adult guidance that encourages learning through play experiences while promoting social, emotional, cognitive, and physical development.
   - How it works: Educators observe and engage in play with children, offering suggestions, questions, or materials to extend their play and support their learning without dictating the direction of the activity.
   - Concrete example: A teacher observes a group of children playing with building blocks and suggests they create a bridge for their toy cars to cross a river made from a large plastic tub filled with water. The children design, build, and test their creation while practicing problem-solving skills, teamwork, and fine motor control.

4. Emergent Curriculum:
   - Definition: A flexible, responsive, and child-centered curriculum that evolves based on the interests, questions, and emerging needs of the children in a particular classroom.
   - How it works: Educators observe children's play, interactions, and discussions to identify topics or themes that spark curiosity and motivation for learning. They then design lessons, activities, and assessments around these areas of interest.
   - Concrete example: After observing that several children are interested in dinosaurs, a teacher creates a multi-week unit focusing on different types of dinosaurs, their habitats, and the geological time periods during which they lived. The curriculum may include various activities such as art projects, science experiments, and field trips to reinforce learning.

EXAM QUESTIONS:
  Q1: Which early childhood education philosophy emphasizes child-led, collaborative, and project-based learning?
       A) Reggio Emilia Approach
       B) Montessori Method
       C) HighScope Approach
  Answer: A

  Q2: In Developmentally Appropriate Practice (DAP), educators consider the interests and learning styles of each child when planning lessons. True or False?
       A) True
       B) False
  Answer: A

  Q3: Child-initiated and adult-supported play encourages learning through play experiences while promoting social, emotional, cognitive, and physical development. How does this approach work?
       A) Educators dictate the direction of the activity and provide suggestions for learning
       B) Educators observe and engage in play with children, offering questions and materials to support their learning
       C) Children are left to play without adult interaction
  Answer: B

  Q4: Emergent curriculum is based on the interests, questions, and emerging needs of the children in a particular classroom. What does this approach do?
       A) Implements a fixed, pre-planned curriculum without considering the children's interests
       B) Creates a flexible, responsive, and child-centered curriculum that evolves based on the children's interests
       C) Focuses only on the teacher's interests and ignores the children's questions
  Answer: B

COURSE: Child Psychology in Päiväkoti
Required passes to complete: 17 weeks
────────────────────────────────────────
MATERIAL:
 Child Psychology in Päiväkoti:

1. **Attachment Theory**
   - Definition: Attachment theory is a concept in child psychology that explains an infant's emotional bond with their primary caregiver.
   - How it works: The bond between an infant and their caregiver is formed through repeated interactions and experiences of comfort, safety, and love. This bond provides the child with a secure base from which to explore the world and a safe haven to return to when frightened or overwhelmed.
   - Concrete example: A baby consistently seeks and responds positively to his mother's presence, indicating a secure attachment. When crying, he calms down quickly when she picks him up.

2. **Egocentrism**
   - Definition: Egocentrism is the inability of young children to understand another person's perspective or point of view.
   - How it works: Children under the age of 7 or 8 years old tend to believe that everyone sees the world from their own perspective, unable to distinguish their thoughts from those of others.
   - Concrete example: A child believes that if he closes his eyes, everything in the room also disappears because he cannot yet understand that his visual perception is subjective.

3. **Cognitive Development**
   - Definition: Cognitive development refers to changes and improvements in a child's ability to think, learn, and solve problems over time.
   - How it works: Jean Piaget's theory of cognitive development suggests that children progress through four distinct stages (sensorimotor, preoperational, concrete operational, and formal operational) as they develop their reasoning skills.
   - Concrete example: A preschooler in the preoperational stage struggles to conserve quantity when presented with two identical glasses filled with water, then poured into one taller glass, because he is unable to mentally manipulate the quantity of the liquid.

4. **Social Development**
   - Definition: Social development involves a child's ability to interact effectively and appropriately with others in various social contexts.
   - How it works: Social development is influenced by factors such as family, peers, and cultural norms. Children learn social skills through interactions, observation, and feedback from others.
   - Concrete example: A toddler in a Päiväkoti learns the concept of sharing toys with other children by observing and imitating their teachers' modeling behavior.

EXAM QUESTIONS:
  Q1: According to Attachment Theory, the bond between an infant and their caregiver is formed through repeated interactions and experiences of comfort, safety, and love. This bond provides the child with a secure base from which to explore the world and a safe haven to return to when frightened or overwhelmed. What does this describe?
       A) Egoism
       B) Egocentrism
       C) Attachment Theory
       D) Social Development
  Answer: C

  Q2: In Piaget's theory of cognitive development, a preschooler in the preoperational stage struggles to conserve quantity when presented with two identical glasses filled with water, then poured into one taller glass. Why does he struggle?
       A) Because he understands that the liquid is still the same quantity
       B) Because he is unable to mentally manipulate the quantity of the liquid
       C) Because he believes everyone sees the world from his own perspective
       D) Because he has not yet learned the concept of sharing
  Answer: B

COURSE: Health and Safety in Päiväkoti
Required passes to complete: 9 weeks
────────────────────────────────────────
MATERIAL:
 Title: Health and Safety in Päiväkoti

1. **Risk Assessment** (Avaaminen)
   - Definition: A systematic process of identifying, evaluating, and controlling potential hazards to ensure a safe and healthy work environment.
   - How it works: In the context of Päiväkoti, risk assessment involves identifying activities or situations that could potentially harm children or staff, assessing the level of risk, and implementing measures to mitigate or eliminate those risks.
   - Concrete Example: A risk assessment might identify a tripping hazard in the play area due to loose floor tiles. The solution could be to either repair the tiles or cover the area with a protective mat until they can be repaired.

2. **Infection Prevention and Control** (Infektiöiden estäminen ja hallinta)
   - Definition: A set of practices designed to prevent infection in the Päiväkoti environment, including hand hygiene, use of personal protective equipment, and management of ill children.
   - How it works: Infection prevention measures might include regular hand washing for staff and children, using gloves during diaper changes, and keeping sick children away from others until they are no longer contagious.
   - Concrete Example: If a child develops a fever, they should be kept separate from other children to prevent the spread of illness. Staff should wear protective clothing and practice frequent hand hygiene when caring for the sick child.

3. **Emergency Procedures** (Asteet urgoksi)
   - Definition: A set of procedures designed to ensure a swift and effective response in case of an emergency, such as a fire or accident.
   - How it works: Emergency procedures might include evacuation plans, first aid training for staff, and procedures for contacting emergency services.
   - Concrete Example: In the event of a fire, children and staff should be evacuated to a designated meeting place outside the building. Staff should be trained in how to use fire extinguishers and call the emergency services.

4. **Child Protection** (Lasten suojelu)
   - Definition: Measures taken to ensure the physical, emotional, and sexual safety of children within the Päiväkoti environment.
   - How it works: Child protection measures might include thorough background checks for staff, policies for reporting and investigating suspected abuse, and training for staff on recognizing signs of abuse and neglect.
   - Concrete Example: A policy for child protection might state that any allegations of abuse must be reported to the appropriate authorities immediately, and that a thorough investigation will be conducted in accordance with local laws and regulations.

EXAM QUESTIONS:
  Q1: What is a systematic process of identifying, evaluating, and controlling potential hazards to ensure a safe and healthy work environment?
       A) Infection Prevention and Control
       B) Risk Assessment
       C) Child Protection
       D) Emergency Procedures
  Answer: B

  Q2: Which of the following is a concrete example of Infection Prevention and Control?
       A) If a child develops a fever, they should be kept separate from other children to prevent the spread of illness.
       B) A risk assessment might identify a tripping hazard in the play area due to loose floor tiles.
       C) In the event of a fire, children and staff should be evacuated to a designated meeting place outside the building.
       D) A policy for child protection might state that any allegations of abuse must be reported to the appropriate authorities immediately.
  Answer: A

MATERIAL:
 Play-Based Learning Strategies in Päiväkoti (Finnish Daycare)

1. **Free Play**
   - Definition: Free play is an unstructured, self-directed learning activity where children are allowed to explore and interact with their environment freely, without adult intervention or predefined goals.
   - How it works: In free play, children are encouraged to use their imagination, creativity, and problem-solving skills to engage in activities of their own choice. This fosters independence, self-regulation, and social skills.
   - Concrete example: A child builds a tower using blocks without any specific instructions or end goal, learning about balance, structure, and creativity in the process.

2. **Dramatic Play**
   - Definition: Dramatic play, also known as role-play or make-believe, is a type of play where children act out scenarios, often pretending to be adults or characters from stories.
   - How it works: Through dramatic play, children develop language skills, empathy, and understanding of social roles and relationships. It also encourages creativity and imagination.
   - Concrete example: Children dress up as doctors and nurses in a pretend hospital, learning about medical roles while practicing communication and cooperation.

3. **Block Play**
   - Definition: Block play involves constructing structures using various types of blocks or building toys. It is often categorized under construction play.
   - How it works: Block play promotes spatial reasoning, problem-solving skills, and mathematical concepts like geometry, measurement, and estimation. It also encourages collaboration and social interaction.
   - Concrete example: Children work together to build a city with different types of blocks, discussing shapes, sizes, and strategies for construction along the way.

4. **Sensory Play**
   - Definition: Sensory play involves activities that stimulate a child's senses, particularly touch, smell, taste, sight, and hearing. It is often messy and open-ended.
   - How it works: Sensory play helps children develop their senses, motor skills, and understanding of the world around them. It also promotes creativity and exploration.
   - Concrete example: Children explore a tray filled with cornstarch and water (also known as "oobleck"), learning about its unique properties while using their hands to mold, squish, and shape it.

EXAM QUESTIONS:
  Q1: What is free play in the context of Play-Based Learning Strategies in Päiväkoti (Finnish Daycare)?
       A) A structured, adult-led learning activity with predefined goals.
       B) An unstructured, self-directed learning activity where children are allowed to explore and interact with their environment freely.
       C) A type of play where children act out scenarios pretending to be adults or characters from stories.
       D) A sensory play involving activities that stimulate a child's senses.
  Answer: B

  Q2: What is one advantage of block play in the context of Play-Based Learning Strategies in Päiväkoti (Finnish Daycare)?
       A) It promotes social interaction and collaboration.
       B) It helps children develop their senses, motor skills, and understanding of the world around them.
       C) It encourages creativity and imagination but does not promote spatial reasoning or problem-solving skills.
       D) It develops language skills, empathy, and understanding of social roles and relationships.
  Answer: A

Required passes to complete: 10 weeks

MATERIAL:
 Päiväkoti Administration: Core Concepts

1. **Päiväkoti (Daycare Center)**
   - Definition: A child care facility that provides all-day care for children while their parents are working or attending school.
   - Function: To provide a safe, nurturing environment for children, promote early childhood development, and support the needs of working parents.
   - Example: Helsinki City Daycare Center is a Päiväkoti that offers care for infants, toddlers, and preschool-aged children. It follows the national curriculum and employs qualified staff to ensure high-quality care.

2. **Curriculum**
   - Definition: A planned sequence of experiences designed to help children learn and develop in a structured, organized way.
   - Function: To guide teachers in planning activities that promote children's cognitive, physical, social, and emotional growth.
   - Example: The Finnish National Core Curriculum for Early Childhood Education and Care outlines learning goals and outcomes for children aged 0-6 years. It focuses on areas such as wellbeing, learning to learn, and competence in various subject areas.

3. **Risk Assessment**
   - Definition: The systematic identification and evaluation of risks or potential hazards within a Päiväkoti and the implementation of strategies to mitigate these risks.
   - Function: To ensure the safety and wellbeing of children, staff, and visitors in the daycare center.
   - Example: Conducting regular fire drills, ensuring that all play equipment is age-appropriate and safe, implementing strict hygiene protocols, and providing first aid training for staff are examples of risk assessment strategies in a Päiväkoti.

4. **Parental Involvement**
   - Definition: The active participation of parents in the daycare center's activities, decision-making processes, and their child's learning and development.
   - Function: To foster open communication between parents and teachers, promote a sense of community, and ensure that the needs of each child are being met.
   - Example: Regular parent-teacher meetings, sharing children's work and progress reports with parents, and inviting parents to participate in school events are ways to encourage parental involvement in a Päiväkoti.

       D) A hospital for the treatment of sick children.
  Answer: C

  Q2: What is the function of a curriculum in a Päiväkoti?
       A) To guide teachers in planning activities that promote children's cognitive, physical, social, and emotional growth.
       B) To sell educational materials to parents.
       C) To determine the number of children each teacher can care for.
       D) To decide the menu for the day.
  Answer: A

MATERIAL:
 Title: Art and Creative Activities for Children in Päiväkoti

1. **Art Education**
   - Definition: A systematic approach to teaching art, emphasizing learning through experience, expression, imagination, and creativity.
   - Function: To develop children's cognitive, emotional, social, and physical skills.
   - Example: Using materials like paints, crayons, and clay to create self-portraits or nature-inspired sculptures.

2. **Creative Dramatics**
   - Definition: A method of teaching theater arts that encourages children's imagination, creativity, and self-expression through dramatic play and storytelling.
   - Function: To develop language skills, improvisation, empathy, and social interaction.
   - Example: Acting out a favorite fairy tale with props and costumes, or creating a simple play based on a group discussion about their day.

3. **Visual Arts**
   - Definition: The creation of works through the use of sight and imagination, often including drawing, painting, sculpture, printmaking, and photography.
   - Function: To enhance children's ability to observe, imagine, and express themselves visually.
   - Example: Creating a collage from magazine cutouts, or designing a poster about an animal they have learned about in Päiväkoti.

4. **Music and Movement**
   - Definition: Activities that combine music with physical movement to stimulate rhythm, coordination, and self-expression.
   - Function: To develop children's sense of rhythm, motor skills, and emotional expression.
   - Example: Dancing to a favorite song, or creating a rhythm using different household items like pots and spoons.
