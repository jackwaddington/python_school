 Play-Based Learning Strategies in Päiväkoti (Finnish Daycare)

1. **Free Play**
   - Definition: Free play is an unstructured, self-directed learning activity where children are allowed to explore and interact with their environment freely, without adult intervention or predefined goals.
   - How it works: In free play, children are encouraged to use their imagination, creativity, and problem-solving skills to engage in activities of their own choice. This fosters independence, self-regulation, and social skills.
   - Concrete example: A child builds a tower using blocks without any specific instructions or end goal, learning about balance, structure, and creativity in the process.

2. **Dramatic Play**
   - Definition: Dramatic play, also known as role-play or make-believe, is a type of play where children act out scenarios, often pretending to be adults or characters from stories.
   - How it works: Through dramatic play, children develop language skills, empathy, and understanding of social roles and relationships. It also encourages creativity and imagination.
   - Concrete example: Children dress up as doctors and nurses in a pretend hospital, learning about medical roles while practicing communication and cooperation.

3. **Block Play**
   - Definition: Block play involves constructing structures using various types of blocks or building toys. It is often categorized under construction play.
   - How it works: Block play promotes spatial reasoning, problem-solving skills, and mathematical concepts like geometry, measurement, and estimation. It also encourages collaboration and social interaction.
   - Concrete example: Children work together to build a city with different types of blocks, discussing shapes, sizes, and strategies for construction along the way.

4. **Sensory Play**
   - Definition: Sensory play involves activities that stimulate a child's senses, particularly touch, smell, taste, sight, and hearing. It is often messy and open-ended.
   - How it works: Sensory play helps children develop their senses, motor skills, and understanding of the world around them. It also promotes creativity and exploration.
   - Concrete example: Children explore a tray filled with cornstarch and water (also known as "oobleck"), learning about its unique properties while using their hands to mold, squish, and shape it.
