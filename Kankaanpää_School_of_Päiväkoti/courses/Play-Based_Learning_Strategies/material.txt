 Play-Based Learning Strategies in Päiväkoti (Finnish Early Childhood Education and Care)

1. **Play as Learning**: This concept emphasizes the educational potential of play. Play is considered a natural means for children to explore, learn, and develop social skills. During play, children can experiment with ideas, solve problems, and interact with their environment in a non-threatening and enjoyable manner.

   Example: A child builds a tower using blocks, learning about balance, gravity, and problem-solving through the process.

2. **Child-Initiated Learning**: This strategy allows children to take the lead in choosing and directing their play activities. The teacher's role is to observe, support, and extend the child's learning rather than dictate it. This approach encourages self-directed learning and fosters independence.

   Example: A teacher provides a variety of materials (paints, clay, dolls) and observes as children select and engage with them in their own way, promoting their individual interests and creativity.

3. **Open-Ended Play**: Open-ended play activities have no specific outcome or end goal. Instead, they provide opportunities for children to express themselves creatively, experiment with different solutions, and develop problem-solving skills.

   Example: Providing loose parts (wooden blocks, pine cones, fabric scraps) encourages children to combine materials in various ways, fostering creativity and inventiveness.

4. **Loose Parts Play**: Loose parts are open-ended play materials that can be moved, carried, combined, redesigned, lined up, taken apart, and put back together in multiple ways. This type of play encourages children to think creatively, develop problem-solving skills, and engage in social interaction.

   Example: A collection of pebbles, sticks, and plastic containers can be used by children to create imaginary landscapes, construct buildings, or design vehicles for role-play scenarios.
